Abstract

Following the recent report of a furin-like cleavage site unique to SARS-CoV-2, we characterized additional amino acid residues, positioned within the crown of the spike (S) glycoprotein, under diversifying selection along the ancestral lineage of currently circulating pandemic strains, and affecting enhanced ability to infect host cells. Molecular dynamic simulations revealed long-range covariant movements of SARS-CoV-2 S glycoprotein monomer in pre-fusion conformation with furin cleavage, and residue T333, under purifying selection, hinge of such movement. Evolutionary analysis revealed that the current human lineage likely emerged after a recombination event between ancestors in bat and pangolin. Recombination impacted residues located in the binding receptor region of S glycoprotein, identical to pangolin ancestor. Interestingly, several recombination hotspots were detected throughout the genome, with the exception of the S glycoprotein, which exhibits a coldspot in the N terminal region, possibly the result of negative selection against changes that may reduce its optimal fitness.

Main

With a toll of confirmed cases rapidly increasing globally, the COVID-19 pandemic is likely to meet the millions in matter of weeks. One of the features that may explain why this novel betacoronavirus (SARS-CoV-2) has been successfully spreading around the globe, is the newly acquired furin-like cleavage site 1 . Furin is ubiquitously expressed at different levels in all tissues 2, 3 . However, kinetics of furin-like enzyme activity are different among species – e. g. human lung cells display quicker kinetic than fruit bat (Pteropus alecto) lung cells 4 – and may not fully explain SARS-CoV-2 enhanced infectivity. On the other hand, we detected several unique amino acid residues that distinguish the spike (S) glycoprotein from ancestral bat and pangolin strains, tested for selective pressure acting on these residues, and further performed homology modeling and molecular dynamic simulations to understand the impact of the cleavage site on the S glycoprotein structure, as it might be modulating infectivity and pathogenicity of the virus 7 . Our results may provide important insights on SARS-CoV-2 infectivity and pathogenesis, as well as for the development of targeted therapies and a vaccine.

SARS-CoV-2 spike (S) glycoprotein features unique to COVID-19

We defined all residues in the new SARS-CoV-2 S glycoprotein unique to the strains circulating among humans by comparing SARS-CoV-2 strains obtained from human, bat, and two pangolin lineages (lineage ‘a’ strains isolated in 2017 in China; ‘b’ strains isolated in 2019 in China) (Table S1). Before diving into the new features, we define major domains of SARS-CoV-2 S glycoprotein, based on the available SARS-CoV-1 structure 5 : S1 subunit, that binds to the receptor on the host cell surface 6 , and S 2 subunit which plays a role in membrane fusion 7 . In particular, S 1 is composed of an N-terminal domain (NTD, residues 14-294) and three C-terminal domains (CTD1, CTD2, and CTD3) ( Figure 1). The CTD1 (residues 320-516 for SARS-CoV and residue 333-529 for SARS-CoV-2, see Methods) functions as the receptor-binding domain (RBD), which allows SARS-CoV-2 to directly bind to the peptidase domain (PD) of angiotensin-converting enzyme 2 (ACE2) 8 ( Figure 1). Within this region, several binding receptor residues and different snapshots of SARS-CoV-2 S glycoprotein have been reported 1, 9 that reflect the dynamic. The recognition step is a dynamic process in which the protein surface of both partners adapt one to the other, in line with cryo-EM evidences that shows how the receptor binding can open up CTD1 10 . For simplicity, we show canonical ACE2 binding residues F486, Q493, S494 and N501, as reported by Andersen et al 1 ( Figure 1). The S 2 subunit is located downstream of S 1 after the S 1/S 2 cleavage site (residue 667 for SARS-CoV and residue 660 for SARS-CoV-2). The majority of the residues that differentiate human S glycoproteins from bat or pangolin were found within the S 1 subunit ( Figure 1a). The S glycoprotein was under strong negative/purifying selection with 775 out of 1273 sites were under negative/purifying selection (Table S3), which suggests the protein has reached a peak on the fitness landscape in terms of structure, stability and folding 11 . We detected six amino acid positions within the S 1 subunit under positive/diversifying selection, likely involved in the adaptation to new host environments ( Figure 1 a, b): positions 32 (S32F), 50 (L50S), 483 (Q483V), and 519 (N519H). Residues 112 (A112S and 352 (K253D) were also under positive selection, but emerged before the split between SARS-CoV-2 human and bat ( Figure 1a). Residue mutation Q483V folds in near proximity to the ACE2 binding domain residues (F496-Q493-S493-N501). It is likely that V483 affects the enhanced ability of currently circulating strains to target human cells ( Figure 1a). In silico prediction of the three-dimensional structure of SARS-CoV-2 S glycoprotein by homology modeling shows that residue mutation Q483V folds in close proximity with residues in the CTD1 domain binding to ACE2 ( Figure 1). All other residues are likely to be positioned in the outside of the S 2 subunit crown, and therefore potentially interacting with receptor of the host cell membrane ( Figure 1 c, d). Interestingly, canonical ACE2 binding residues, conserved between human and pangolin lineage b strains, do not appear to be under negative selection. These mutations may be involved in host adaption, thus leaving with the question of when and how these residues have been acquired.

Polybasic furin cleavage and prefusion conformation

While the S glycoproteins of MERS-CoV, HcoV-OC43, and HKU1 display the canonical (R/K)-(2X)n-(R/K)* (or RXXR*SA) motif for the S1/S2 cleavage 12, 13 , the S glycoprotein of the recently emerged SARS-CoV-2 presents a solvent exposed furin-like cleavage sequence (PRRARS*V) in positions 681-684 1. We carried out a 90 ns-long simulation to assess the stability of the trimeric structure of SARS-CoV-2 and whether the new gained favorable furin-like cleavage site alters the overall structure of the S glycoprotein.

As recently reported 14 , the CTD1 domain of SARS-CoV-2 S glycoprotein for the pre-fusion conformation rotates outward from center of the triangular head to an “up” position, while this conformation for SARS-CoV is “down” 5 ( Figure 1f). One monomer of the SARS-CoV-2 S glycoprotein, in fact, acquired the ability of being already in a pre-fusion conformation pointing the ACE2 binding site in a different angle than its predecessor, thus giving SARS-CoV-2 advantageous ability to bind and enter into the cell. To test whether specific evolutionary pressures allowed SARS-CoV-2 to display enhanced binding to the human binding receptor, we performed molecular dynamic (MD) simulations. Projection of the MD trajectory along the first eigenvector, carried out by Essential Dynamics analysis 15 , shows a clear up/down (opening/closing) mechanism of the SARS-CoV-2 monomer by means of the rotation of CTD1 (see Figure 1e and Suppl. Movies 1-2). MD simulations highlighted T333 (under negative/purifying selection), its first residue, as the hinge of the movement. It is worth noting that the region 325-344 (mostly under negative/purifying selection) is completely conserved between SARS-CoV-2 and closely related bat and pangolin strains ( Figure 1a), in line with a key role of this region in guiding the opening/closing of CTD1. The same analysis shows a correlated movement between CTD1 (residues between red lines in Figure 1f) and the region 828-852 (highlighted by cyan lines in Figure 1 f), encompassing a portion of the fusion peptide (Suppl. Movie 3, in which residues 828-852 are highlighted in cyan). This co-variant movement between CTD1 and the fusion peptide region is indicative of long range coordinated movements, functional to the protein rearrangement during the fusion step ( Figure 1f, Suppl. Movie 3). A correlated movement (peak of fluctuation) is also observed in the residues 699-718 (orange lines in Figure 1f) that comprehend furin-like cleavage site typical of SARS-CoV-2. This region shows a covariance in its movement with residues 460-496 in CTD1 and 250-263 in NTD (Suppl. movie 4, in which residues 828-852 are highlighted in orange color).

On the origin of SARS-CoV-2 lineage: recombination

While the origin of this virus strain remains unknown, numerous scenarios have been proposed 1, 16, 17 . Recombination is an important factor driving viral diversity 18, 19 , and has been reported as a possible mechanism favoring cross-species transmission of CoV and increasing its adaptability to a new host 20, 21 . Recombination was a critical factor in the emergence of Middle East respiratory syndrome coronavirus (MERS-CoV) in 2012 from bat reservoirs 22, 23 . There is currently conflicting evidence on whether or not the new coronavirus originated from recombination events 24- 26 . Since the human lineages is 96% identical to bat-SARS-CoV-2 1 , but ACE2 binding residues F486, Q493, S494 and N501 ( Figure 1a) are conserved between pangolin-SARS-CoV-2 lineage b strains, we investigated the evolutionary relationships among available full genome sequences (bat and pangolin-related SARS-CoV-2 strains, bat-SL-CoVs, SARS-CoV-1 and SARS-like strains) using a neighbor network (NNet) based algorithm 27 . NNets have the advantage over phylogeny inference algorithms of allowing internal nodes to be cyclically connected (nets) to represent the presence of conflicting phylogenetic signal in the data (i.e. data support for different tree topologies), as in fact we found in the analysis of the coronavirus genomes ( Fig. 2a). Of course, conflicting phylogenetic signal in the data is not sufficient to infer recombination 28 . To test whether the NNet topology is due to recombination we used the pairwise homoplasy index (PHI) test 29 , which detected highly significant signal for recombination ( p <; 10 −99) among all included strains ( Figure 1a). SARS-CoV-1 and human SARS-CoV-2 monophyletic clades do not display net-like topology, and the PHI test on a sub-dataset including only these lineages was not significant ( p = 0.08). SARS-like and bat-SL-CoVs, on the other hand, show a high degree of conflicting phylogenetic signals reflected by intricate networks ( Fig. 1a). While SARS-like strains are ancestors of SARS-CoV-1 strains, bat-SL-CoVs stand as intermediate link between SARS-CoV-1 and SARS-CoV-2 strains. Bat-SARS-CoV-2 (EPI_ISL_402131, isolated from bat in 2013 in China) and pangolin-SARS-CoV-2 strains (EPI_ISL_410538-43 strains isolated in 2017 in China, and EPI_ISL_412860 and EPI_ISL_410721 strains isolated in 2019 in China, indicated with “a” and “b”, respectively, in Figure 2a) are ancestors of SARS-CoV-2 circulating in humans. When testing sub-sets of the alignment including only SARS-CoV-2 and its ancestors, the PHI test still indicated strong evidence of recombination (p = 1.9×10 −8), although a weaker signal for recombination was detected for SARS-CoV-2 with pangolin-SARS-CoV-2 ancestral strains (p = 0.013). Significant recombination signal was also detected (p = 6.6×10 −4) testing SARS-CoV-2 bat (EPI_ISL_402131) and pangolin strains. As it is not possible to conclude whether pangolin, bat or human SARS-CoV-2 strains have been recombining based on PHI test, we scanned whole genomes for putative recombination breakpoints using six algorithms 30 that allow the identification of specific sets of recombinant as well as parental strains (when present in the sampled sequences). A total of 68 potential recombination events distributed along the genome were found to affect bat-SL-CoVs, SARS-CoVs and SARS-like CoVs strains (Table S4). Human SARS-CoV-2 strains were identified as potential parental sequences recombining with pangolin-SARS-CoV-2 isolated in 2019 and bat-SARS-CoV-2 strains; with a recombination event involving the region of the S glycoprotein that binds to the cell receptor ( Table 1). This finding matches the previous observation that ACE2 binding residues are shared between pangolin and human, and the recent analysis by Li et al 26 . While our analysis could not confidently discern the recombinant from the parent sequences, signature pattern analysis ( Figure 1a) and a Maximum likelihood tree calculated using this recombinant region only (data not shown), clearly showed pangolin and COV-2 clustering together. Our overall findings point to the conclusion that a recombination event between ancestors of human SARS-CoV-2, or a closely related to bat-SARS-CoV-2, and ancestors of pangolin-SARS-CoV-2 lineage b strains, is the likely origin of the currently circulating pandemic strains.

Using coordinates of identified recombination breakpoints, we plotted their distribution and analyzed it through permutations to predict hot- and coldspots of recombination across the coronavirus genomes ( Figure 2b). A first iteration, performed with a sliding window of size 100, identified nine potential recombination hotspots. Seven of them were found in the polyprotein segment ( Figure 2b), which spans three quarters of the viral RNA; the remaining two involved protein E, orf8, and protein N. It has been reported that the orf8 gene plays a role in regulating the viral replication in the host cell 31 , and was probably acquired by the whole lineage of SARS-CoV-1 from the SARS-related CoV from Greater Horseshoe bats (SARSr-Rf-BatCoVs) following a recombination event with a Chinese horseshoe bat coronavirus (SARSr-Rs-BatCoV) 32 . A second analysis, with a sliding window of size 200, detected two recombination coldspots on the N terminal region of the glycoprotein (between residues S31 to V47, and Y145 to G184, p ≤0.01) ( Figure 2b). Under more relax assumptions (0.01 ≤ p ≤0.05) coldspot region spans from Q14 to S71, and from V127 to T240 ( Figure 2b). Residues S32F, A112S, N519H ( Figure 1) are outside the coldspot region, however L50S is inside the first coldspot region of SARS-CoV-2 S glycoprotein. Our finding seems to be in conflict with high recombination rates being found in non-human coronaviruses 33 , yet, it is not surprising that part of a gene playing a fundamental role in cell entry and fusion would sit into a recombination coldspot. While the majority of the CoV genome seems prone to recombination, the S glycoprotein is the only one that harbored recombination coldspots. This might depend on functional costraints on specific domains of the protein 34 , or other factors impacting the probability of recombination events, such as RNA secondary structure 35, 36 . Another possibility is that the N terminal, under negative selection, reached a peak on the adaptive landscape and new mutations/rearrangements are unlikely to be fixed in the next generations due to negative selection. These findings correlate with mobility of MD simulations, where flexible stretches are less conserved, while hinge regions remained unchanged during evolution of the lineage.

4 Conclusions

We provided molecular insights on SARS-CoV-2 infectivity and pathogenesis and identified potential targets for therapies, in the context of coronaviruses evolutionary trajectory that has led to the current pandemic.

SARS-CoV-2 S glycoprotein presents two notable changes: a favorable furin-like cleavage site 1 , and CTD1 up in pre-fusion mode 14 . On the contrary S glycoprotein of previously circulating SARS-CoV strains do not have a favorable furin-like cleavage site 1 and remain un-cleaved after biosynthesis 37 . Furin has the potential to cleave specifically viral envelope glycoproteins, to enhance viral fusion with host cell membranes 38 , thereby any variation/mutational changes in amino acid composition at cleavage sites may impact tissue and cell tropism, host range, and pathogenesis 39 , as reported for other respiratory viruses 40- 42 . Moreover, our MD simulations correlated furin-like cleavage site correlated with pre-fusion conformation. Simulations indicated that the motion of CTD1 is quite visible and is the most relevant motion in the protein: a conserved region that acts as a hinge in the opening/closing mechanism. We described residue T333, under purifying selection, as the hinge of the rotation movement that gives further advantage to bind cell receptors facilitating fusion and entry. Further analysis will be done to analyze the conformation change of the S glycoprotein and the residues interface in nearly real conditions. Development of inhibitors of such motion may reduce substantially pre-fusion conformation and therefore the infectivity of SARS-CoV-2.

SARS-CoV-2 S glycoprotein gained specific features that have facilitated the successful spread of the virus as compared to its predecessors. The assumption that the insertion of a furin cleavage site allowed a bat CoV to gain the ability to infect humans facilitating the host specie jump once the conditions were favorable cannot be excluded. By structure similarity with S glycoprotein of mouse hepatitis virus, it is possible that SARS-CoV-2 also uses distal loops of the N-terminal domain to interact with cell receptor 43 . Although more evidence is needed, it is conceivable that residues in this region that are under positive selection may have been selected to immune-modulate the host. Several recombination events involving ancestral pangolin- and bat-SARS-CoV-2 lineages. According to our results, one of these events led to the current configuration of SARS-CoV-2 ACE2 binding residues, including recently described residues forming hydrogen bonds with the host cell receptor 9 . In spite of the frequency of recombination events involving the S glycoprotein, the 5’ end of the gene might sit into a recombination coldspot. While mutation/recombination events in the glycoprotein RBD might expand the virus tropism, recombination coldspots and negative/diversifying selective pressure limit its variability to maintain correct structure, and therefore function. RDB is definitively a good target for therapeutic development, while regions in coldspots or undergoing negative/diversifying selective pressure may be helpful for successful vaccine design. Our candidate in silico recombination hotspots and coldspots findings need to be further investigated with in vitro experiments.

We conclude that SARS-CoV-2 likely emerged from recombination events that involves ancestral strains circulating among bat and pangolin. However, we could not completely distinguish the recombinant sequence from the parental ones in other cases. Recombination signal and selective pressures can interfere with each other 44 , with positive selected mutations near potential recombination breakpoints 45 . More extensive sampling of wildlife CoVs would help in clarifying the events that led to the jump to humans. Future emergence of novel recombinant CoVs, and other viruses as well, should be a constant concern; strengthening surveillance of viral populations and improving our understanding of their dynamics will help the prevention of new zoonotic outbreaks and spread of emerging pathogens.

Methods

Genetic data

Publicly available genomic CoVs sequences were obtained from GenBank and GISAID (Table S1 and S5). To gather closely related CoVs BLASTn was performed using the ViPR database and keeping the first 100 hits. Genome sequences were aligned using MAFFT and refined manually 49 . Alignments for the glycoprotein were extracted from the genome alignment.

Protein selection and signature pattern

Because of the fast rate at which new genomes have been deposited in GISAID, this analysis was updated to 728 genomes (Table S5). While in the recombination analysis the number of genomes is not relevant, as they are very similar to each other, for selection analysis and identification of new features, a higher number of strains permits to explore a larger evolutionary landscape. HyPhy 46 algorithm mixed effects model of evolution (MEME) 47 and Fast Unconstrained Bayesian AppRoximation (FUBAR) 48 were used to estimate the ratio of non-synonymous ( dN) to synonymous ( dS) substitution rates for each codon. MEME was employed to identify individual sites subject to episodic diversifying selection, and FUBAR for inferring site-specific pervasive selection. Sites were considered to have experienced statistically significant positive or negative selection based on the following cut-offs: LRT ≤ 0.05 or posterior probability (PP) >; 0.90, for MEME and FUBAR respectively. VESPA algorithm 49 was used to detect the residue signature pattern distinguishing SARS-CoV-2 strains from the closely related bat (EPI_ISL_402131, 2013, Accession MN996532.1) and pangolin (EPI_ISL_410538, 2017; EPI_ISL_410539, 2017; EPI_ISL_410540, 2017; EPI_ISL_410541, 2017; EPI_ISL_410542, 2017; EPI_ISL_410543, 2017; EPI_ISL_412860, 2019; EPI_ISL_410721, 2019) strains. Positions are numbered with SARS-CoV-2 isolate Wuhan-Hu-1 (Accession MN908947.3) as reference.

Structure homology modeling

As a first step, the amino acid sequences of SARS-CoV-2 Wuhan-Hu-1 (Accession QHD43416) and EPI_ISL_402131 (bat, 2013, Accession MN996532.1) were submitted to Swiss-model 50 . The best models showing high values of Global Model Quality Estimation (GMQE) were selected and used as templates. Amino acid alignments of targets and templates were performed using EMBOSS Needle 51 . The 3D homology homo-trimer models were created with the Modeller v9.23 52, 53 automodel class using the alignment as a guide. Then Discreet Optimised Protein Energy Score (DOPE) based model selection 54 and refinements were conducted using Modeller v9.23 scripts. The final structure model validation was conducted with ProSA 55, 56 and QMEAN servers 57, 58 . Visualization of the atomic models, including figures and movies, is made with Chimera v1.12 59.

Molecular Dynamics simulation

The structure of SARS-CoV-2, obtained for homology modeling, was used as starting structure for Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations. In detail, topology file was generated with the pdb2gmx Gromacs tool 60 , using the amber99sb forcefield 61 .The protein was embedded in a triclinic box, extending up to 15 Å from the solute, and immersed in TIP3P water molecules 62 . Counter ions were added to neutralize the overall charge with the genion gromacs tool. After energy minimizations, the systems were slowly relaxed for 5 ns by applying positional restraints of 1000 kJ mol -1 nm -2 to the protein atoms. Then unrestrained MD simulations were carried out for a length of 90 ns with a time step of 2 fs with GROMACS 2018.3 simulation package (Pall et al., 2015) (supercomputer Galileo, CINECA, Bologna, Italy). V-rescale temperature coupling was employed to keep the temperature constant at 300 K 63 . The Particle-Mesh Ewald method was used for the treatment of the long-range electrostatic interactions 64 . The first 5 ns portion of the trajectory was excluded from the analysis.

We carried out the Essential Dynamics analysis to identify the main 3N directions along which the majority of the protein motion is defined 15 . This analysis is based on the diagonalization of the covariance matrix built from the atomic fluctuations after the removal of the translational and rotational movement and we applied it on the 1120 c-alpha atoms of each monomer (residue 27-1146 in our model), after concatenating their trajectories (255 ns total sampling).

Recombination breakpoint and hotspot analyses

The presence of conflicting phylogenetic signals (i.e. distinct tree topologies compatible with the same set of aligned sequences) in the full genome data set was investigated, first, by inferring networks using neighbor-net 27 implemented in SplitsTree4 28 . Significant presence of recombination signal was then assessed with the pairwise homoplasy index (Phi) test 29 . Identification of putative recombinant events and associated breakpoints was performed using the RDP, GENECONV, MaxChi, CHIMAERA, SIScan, and 3Seq algorithms implemented in the RDP4 30 software ( http://web.cbio.uct.ac.za/~darren/rdp.html). Statistical evidence of recombination was indicated by p-values <; 0.05, and a putative recombination events were considered as such if supported by at least five of the six algorithms used. Full table with recombination results id given in Table S4.

Recombination breakpoints hotspot analysis was also performed using RDP4 through a permutation test, which takes into account number of variable positions between the three sequences involved in the event when performing simulations. Two iteration were performed, with sliding windows of size 100 and 200, with different sensitivity to hotspots and coldspots. Segments that had more observed breakpoints than the local maximum number of breakpoints of 99% of 1,000 permutaions were considered hotspots (p=0.01); in the same fashion, segments that had less breakpoints than the local minimum of 99% of permutations were considered coldspots 65 .
